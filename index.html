About me

clouds restore in rain
the exhalations of the land and sea
            -H. W. Longfellow

This lovely snippet from Longfellow’s prelude to Michael Angelo describes the water cycle: the movement of water around the Earth.

I am a climate scientist, tackling the same topic but using science. With my research I work to understand the huge range of factors influencing when, where, and why rain falls in different places around the world - and how that is changing because of climate change.

I take a holistic approach to understanding Earth’s water cycle, producing and combining information from weather observations, climate model simulations, and natural archives of climate variability through past centuries (e.g., tree rings and ice cores). I study water cycle behaviour on spatial scales from local to global and on time scales from monthly to multi-centennial.

I am currently a DECRA fellow at The University of Adelaide (Australia). Before that, I was a postdoctoral researcher at the Australian National University (working with the ARC Centre of Excellence for Climate Extremes), and before that a postdoctoral researcher at Washington University in St. Louis.

Aside from climate science, my interests are many and varied: edited highlights include running, swimming, cross country skiing, telemark skiing, spoon-carving, reading fantasy novels, taking bad photos with old film cameras, using the Oxford comma, and playing guitar & violin.

19 December 2024

I’m honoured to have been selected by Science and Technology Australia to participate in the 2025/2026 Superstars of STEM programme.

The programme aims to break down gender assumptions about who can work in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics) and boost media visibility of women and non-binary STEM experts. After a long selection process, myself and 59 other STEM professionals from across Australia - across fields, industries, and career stages - will be supported in appearing as expert commentators on Australian media, on platforms from news and panel shows to social media and public events. The idea is to show the community that a scientist can look like….well, anything. And also to encourage a more diverse range of young people to follow their STEM dreams. This is more important than ever, with Australia facing, horrifyingly, a widening junior- and high-school gender gap in science and mathematics. This gap is, of course, not due to any inherent gender-based difference in STEM abilities, but rather the wildly inaccurate stereotype of STEM being more for boys than girls.

I’m hoping to do my bit to push change - and fly the flag for the critical importance of climate science while I’m at it.

29 OCTOBER 2024

Just out in Weather and Climate Extremes: we show that the severity of Australia’s 2017-2019 ‘Tinderbox’ drought (the one that preceded the 2019/20 bushfires) was likely made worse by climate change.

We use Linear Inverse Models to produce 10,000 years of ‘pseudo-observations’ of Australian rainfall and global SST anomalies, and use these to quantify the full natural range of natural variability in Australian rainfall. Turns out, rainfall deficits over the Murray-Darling Basin during the Tinderbox Drought fall outside that range. This was largely driven by the very severe deficits in 2019, which co-occurred with a strong positive Indian Ocean Dipole event.

We also looked into the relationship of Murray-Darling Basin rainfall and global SST anomalies more broadly. We show that the most reliable predictors of very dry years in the Murray-Darling Basin are positive SSTs in the central tropical Pacific and western Indian Oceans. Both maps in the figure below show characteristic anomalies during the driest 5th percentile of years in the Murray-Darling Basin.

To look at that another way, the driest years in the Murray-Darling Basin are most reliably (although not always) associated with positive anomalies in the Nino 3.4 and Dipole Mode indices. In the plot below, the grey cloud shows the full distribution of co-occurring Nino 3.4 and DMI anomalies in our 10,000 LIM years. The blue cloud shows the subset of those years where MDB rainfall was in the lowest 5th percentile.

Read the paper here and please feel free to get in touch with any questions. The LIM outputs are freely available from this Zenodo repository

28 MARCH 2024

Out today in Hydrology and Earth System Sciences is our assessment of multi-year droughts in climate model simulations of the last millennium.

We looked at Australian drought characteristics in 11 climate models, including how simulated droughts in pre-Industrial times compared with 20th century droughts. We found that on average, droughts in SE and SW Australia are getting longer.

But also, natural variability in Australian rainfall could bring 20+ year droughts - including to the food bowl of Australia (the Murray-Darling Basin).

We also show that the short observational era isn’t long enough to get the full picture around the statistics of multi-year droughts - highlighting the need for more targeted palaeoclimate data.

Read the paper here and please feel free to get in touch with any questions.

6 MARCH 2024

Just published in Science Advances: our extremely comprehensive analysis of the development, drivers, and impacts of south-eastern Australia’s 2017-2019 ‘Tinderbox’ Drought - the one that set the conditions for the 2019 Black Summer fires.

The paper is the culmination of a glorious collaboration between the ARC Centre of Excellence for Climate Extremes’ finest drought scientists, and covers everything from agriculture and vegetation impacts to remote drivers and climate change contribution.
